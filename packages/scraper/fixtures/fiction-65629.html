Could you survive a horror movie?

That's the question Riley must answer after he and his friends get lured to Carousel, a malevolent town where horror movies come to life.

Riley thinks he is in big trouble when he is assigned the Film Buff archetype, a minor support class that has a penchant for dying early in movies. At first resigned to his fate to die over and over, he believes he has found a way to survive. Upon obtaining the Oblivious Bystander trope, monsters and killers will not harm him as long as he can convincingly pretend he has not noticed them. At first, this ability appears to be a joke, but Riley thinks that with some clever exploitation, it might just be his greatest strength.

What to expect:

-Strategic gameplay and clever uses of movie tropes to survive

-Perilous storylines right from your favorite horror movies

-Creative Twists of Classic Horror Movie tropes

-Moderate stats-based combat and strategy

Masterpiece in the Making

Reviewed at: Book Five, Chapter 68: Silverware
by Ratchet_Sly Thursday, March 20, 2025 8:40:38 PM

This story is the B2 bomber to the Wright brother's biplane, pure innovation. It takes litrpg, distills its essence, and transforms it into a creation that works for this particular setting.

 

Speaking of the setting, it is incredibly unique and fleshed out. The world of Carousel is all at once both known to the reader as the prison/madhouse it openly portrays, but is at the same time a complete mystery that shows we don't entirely know what all the moving pieces are or what they mean.

 

I would say character development isn't quite where I would expect it to be. Perhaps that is a symptom of how stories on Royal Road are released, 1 chapter at a time. However, prior to web novels, I've read completed and published novels that suffer from the same, so who's to say.

 

I don't like to compare and rate particular things I enjoy like books and food. I feel it diminishes the joy I get from eating a great pizza or a perfect medium rare steak if I have to place one above another. Sames goes for novels. Having said that, in the mental library where I keep all the books I've enjoyed, lost_rambler's The Game at Carousel has a home.

Reviewed at: Chapter Ninety-Five: The Casks and the Crime Scene
by The_Lost_Void Thursday, August 24, 2023 4:39:08 AM

This novel does something very rare; it actually makes great use of humanities will to survive and great intellect. Typically, in a lot of fantasies like this one, the characters are a slave to the plot, however, in this we get to see the side of humanity that wants to exploit everything. Without spoiling the story too much, essentially the survivors in this story treat their experiences much like a video game forum. They pool together information, and try to wring out every bit possible, even if it means trying to break whatever magic keeps them there. It makes me happy to see that not every character is an idiot. That said, unlike a lot of video games where you can quite literally test every pixel of the game, in Carousel you are given far less freedom to test things out. To all those who are frustrated that the characters aren't exploiting their abilities enough, the book explains that soon enough.

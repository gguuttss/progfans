What does it take to climb out of the trash and into the sky?

The very heavens had decreed his death. He should have died in the womb. Or in infancy. Or when his family was murdered. Or when the house burned around him. Or when he woke up in the dump suffering from innumerable diseases and missing fingers. Even the magic ring with a ghostly grandpa trapped inside of it was sabotaged. A reasonable person would roll over and die. Tian isn't that reasonable. And as it happens, neither is Grandpa Jun. In a world where immortality can be cultivated, miracles are things you make for yourself. A broken boy will defy the heavens, and soar.

What to expect:

-1 chapter a day M-F. Volume 5 is already complete.

-2k words per chapter (aprox, some run long) with a longer first chapter.

-No Harem

-Earned power growth

-An MC who isn't a goodie two shoes OR an edge-lord psychopath

-A power system based on actual Daoism 

-Sects, giant monsters, natural treasures, flying swords, spells, talismans, secret arts, and a gentle sprinkling of young masters whose face need rearranging.

Read it, trust me.

Reviewed at: Chapter 23- A Yin Man
by Noroh Thursday, January 29, 2026 8:39:08 AM

Please just read Sky Pride instead of my review. I'm going to tell you why you should read it, and go into detail about what makes it good, but honestly reading my review is just time you could have spent reading this gem of a novel.

Style: If I wasn't almost certain that the author's first language was English I would think this was a very modern work translated excellently. The story is steeped in Xianxia lore, seamlessly incorperating elements from classic cultivation stories, ancient Chinese classics, and even real history (with the bar codes sanded off). The result is a world that feels rich in history and full of magic, but still alive outside of our main character. People live their lives along the rivers and lakes of the Broadsky Kingdom, they have traditions and legends, feuds and marriages, rights and obligations. Daoist thought pervades the whole work, grounding the cultivation logic without making it feel too rigid or shallow. The Five Elemental Phases, Yin and Yang, Vital Energy, Chi, and more are all naturally revealed over the course of the story though our main character Tian Zihao. The story is primarily 3rd person limited on Tian, allowing the reader to learn about the world with him, without jarring lore dumps.

Story: Most Cultivation stories center around a main character who has the biggest fist. Tian Zihao can't even make a fist when we find him. His journey is not simply one of a boy defying the heavens and usurping the Mad God, it is a quest to become human and humane in a world that is often remarkably cruel. As I noted in the style section Daoism suffuses the story, but not simply in a mechanical way. Rather Daoist thought is used by Tian to understand himself and the world around him. Understanding Fire or Wood is not simply a power up for Tian, although he might become stronger for it, rather it is in understanding what Fire or Wood means to him that truly drives the story. How to endure and overcome hardship, how to deal with justified anger and reasonable hate, what it means to be virtuous, what friendship is, what a father is, what to do when something or someone you love hurts you very badly. All this wrapped in a action packed, dry witted, offbeat xianxia.

Grammar: I have seen comments asking for the original Chinese scans, in truth or in jest I cannot say. While I think comparing it to a translated work is fair in terms of style it is consistantly solid grammar-wise. There are a few issues here and there, the most common being using the wrong character's name in a dialogue once every few hundred chapters or so. The Oxford comma is not used, which is a style thing not a grammar thing but I put it here because people think it is a grammar thing. There is also some phrasing that might seem odd or stilted if you are unfamiliar with translated works from Japan or China. This occurs most often when honorifics or other polite language is used. There are also several Chinese euphemisms and idioms that are used, as well as the occasional pun in Chinese, which are usually explained diagetically but could be confused for mistakes.

Characters: All the Characters are fantastic, fleshed out and fully realized, several of them could (and should) be the main characters of their own novels. Tian Zihao is a delight, beginning almost completly feral, and never truly losing that edge, he has a core of kindness that the world with all its cruelty has yet to beat out of him. Despite how many bad things happen to him he finds time to take joy and revel in what he can see and learn and do. When he improves he is estatic, when he struggles he pushes through with grit. He is clever, hard working, and very, very hurt. Obviously suffering from his circumstances, but always trying to get better, physically, mentally, and spiritually. The world is full of Rock Throwers, Tian wants to be better than them, but he will not simply let them stone him either. Grampa Jun is a wonderful ghost, offering gentle wisdom and familial comfort in Tian's darkest moments. All of the cast, Senior Brother Fu, Auntie Bai, Hong Liren, Librarian Su, Fatty Wang, Sister Lin, Daoist Shu, Auntie Wu, Censor Henshen, no matter how long they are present for feel vibrant and alive. Tian's adventures ripple outward from him to them and their own journeys ripple back to Tian.

TL;DR: See my opening statement.

Reasonable quality, very readable

Reviewed at: Chapter 20- The Delicate Art of Enmity
by W. Ku Thursday, July 10, 2025 6:34:21 PM

I'm a decent way in, certainly enough to have a good feel for the story, and I'm calmly enjoying it.

The writing style is solid - the author can tell a story without overwriting it or doing anything that particularly grates or jars, which is no small task. It is paced and structured in a way that flows, although there isn't much of a build-up-and-payoff to the fight scenes - they just sort of happen as and when, and the MC never really seems to struggle.

There are occasional moments of safely-done humour, but I wouldn't describe the style as having any particular flair or notable sense of individuality. There is nuance of wording, but never those monents of "oh, I see what this writer's doing here, and I like it!". That said, it is solid and readable - and with a strong story and characters and premise, that can be the best possible thing. A telling, with no distractions. 

The characters, for the most part, are archetypal, with some depth to MC and mentor. MC is naive and wary of his own naiveté despite needing to make his way in the world somehow, which is a nice source of inner conflict, but it hasn't played much part as yet - he rarely agonises or deliberates for more than sentence or two before doing anything with conplete confidence, whcih turns out to be the right thing. This is moderated by the mentor character Grandpa, who remains mysterious yet central - this is a nice mechanic and creates curiosity and a  sense of a mystery to be slowly unravelled. There are few other characters as yet, and they are largely functional and simple rather than objects of intrigue per se. 

The plot generates a lot of synpathy for the MC as well as some mystery surrounding his origin story and some foreshadowing of parallel plots of cultivation and vengeance. A lot seems to conveniently happen as and when needed to advance the story - the tiger, the lotus-bath - but not gratuitously so, and it comes with cool imagery and concepts, so I'm more than happy to overlook that minor thing for the sake of a well-paced story with continuous progression.

A lot of depth and detail in the worldbuilding and magic system has been hinted at, but not yet enplored. I like this. It's a difficult balance between oversimplicity/shallowness and overloading a reader who has no reason to care yet, and it's struck well here. There is definitely going to be depth, but not yet. That's a good reader feeling. 

Definitely going to keep reading. I can't tell yet whether it's going to carry on being cool-but-not-mindblowing, or whether it's suddenly going to exlode with depth and intrigue late into the book, like Magician (Feist) or Eragon (Paolini).

Reviewed at: Chapter 36- Burning Bright
by Eli70G Tuesday, April 28, 2026 12:33:54 PM

This story starts and has many elements of a typical XianXia story, but quickly sifones itself as so much more. 

I’ve learned more about policies from ancient China and their impact on people from this story than any history class. The author frequently provides his source for the major items in the author’s note and encourages you to read up on what was done and the consequences.

Something as simple as “Who should be in charge of selling salt?” Becomes the major driving force for the conflicts in the first hour books. And I love it for that.

The main character just feels right.

This xianxia novel stands out from the crowd. It’s not just another revenge fantasy where every slight leads to mass destruction. Instead, it delivers a thoughtful and balanced story, where conflicts are meaningful and the world feels alive.

The protagonist's growth is handled with care—both in terms of power and personality. You watch as he learns, struggles, and evolves, making each breakthrough feel earned rather than handed to him.

If you're tired of one-dimensional cultivation stories, this one offers a refreshing change. It has action, depth, and a main character you can genuinely root for.

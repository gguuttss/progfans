Original COMPLETED Adventure Historical Mystery Romance Subplot

In May, 1899, a hundred and fifty unassuming envelopes are delivered to households across the city. Most of them will be picked up by butlers and carried on silver trays to their recipients. All of them contain invitations to the wedding of Lady Eleanor Serrs to Mr. Ryce Penn. When the newspapers hear about it, they start buzzing. Who can blame them? This would be the wedding of the century! She’s the daughter of Duke Erravold Aubrey-Serrs, and the Marvelous Mr. Penn is one of the most famous men in the country.

But Penn is famous for being the city’s most notorious thief. He’s an unrepentant attention-seeker with a taste for gems and danger. And Eleanor swears that she’s never met him.

Chief Inspector Lucas Haley, Penn’s long-time nemesis, is called in to protect the Aubrey-Serrs family and try to figure out what the master thief is planning. He’ll need all his experience and tenacity for this case, as it quickly becomes obvious more is at stake than the reputation of an innocent woman.

Delightful. Marvelous. Charming. Witty. Lasting.

Reviewed at: Epilogue
by ShyBoy Sunday, December 14, 2025 6:46:51 PM

Quality writing, paperback quality at that. You will not regret reading this, there's much to enjoy.

Brilliant characterisation where every character has flaws (yes, more than one). Interactions between characters are a great study for not only high society manners, subtlety, or reading between the lines. Here, they are often shedding light on human condition in general. More than once I studied the dialogues or actions between characters just for the art of writing that birthed them. And, of course, even more so, the art of characterisation. A sentiment I heard shared about this novel is that on a scale of 1-5 the characters here deserve a 6, for the rest is indeed very good but characters are even better. I can see that. I do agree with that.

Excellent adherence to show don't tell, twists that surprise, and a villain... Ah, I cannot risk this. My apologies, but I honestly cannot risk this reveal. Nothing shall be said about the villain, except that the choice and writing and timing were very crafty, very crafty indeed.

This is a treat, ladies and gentlemen. This, is a hidden treasure, which even our Marvelous Master Thief, Mr. Penn, Esquire, didn't, or rather wouldn't loot.

We have here Arsene Lupin vibes, for there's really well done inspector - thief dynamic where you really aren't fully sure who will win and how they'll prevail.

We have a country that will very well play with Victorian Era fans.

We have Austen-like powers of observations, exuded by several characters, that doesn't get boring, for each of them does it in their own manner, through their own lenses, therefore quite uniquely. And each also has certain gotcha moments, where they were simply blindsided. And therefore every single one of them is very human.

The style fits like a glove to: mystery investigation genre, criminal detective stories, high-society romances, as well as gentleman thief adventures. And gazette-published letters. Which is what The Unexpected Engagement of the Marvelous Mr. Penn is and what RKGoff so well employs in her storytelling. Thanks to that style, even banter was a refreshing deviation from the usual one-liners, tired in their overuse.

Very much a refreshing outlier here on RR. You should not miss it. 5s all around.

A marvelous adventure

Reviewed at: Epilogue
by TwoChaptersAndADream Saturday, March 25, 2023 2:13:16 AM

Highly entertaining cop and robber story set in turn of the 19-20th century not- England. I don't get why it hasn't gotten more attention. If you're debating trying it,  give the first two chapters a chance! You won't regret it.  

The story begins as unlikely as it seems straightforward.  After stealing gold,  gems and people's dignity,  a charismatic thief chooses an unconventional target for his next major heist: namely our protagonists' hand (in marriage). But not all is as it appears, and as you read you will probably suspect most of the colorful cast of wrongdoings at least once. Along the way a young woman finds her voice, an unconventional love blooms and there is even a murder. My only slight disappointment is that there wasn't a little more foreshadowing at the end of this whodunnit, as for me the twist came as quite a surprise!

Still though,  what a wonderful, nay, marvelous adventure!

The practical components of this story are also excellent. Grammar is perfect,  I spotted no flaws,  and the word choice matched the time and the place of the setting. The style itself is great,  and the pacing is excellent. The characters are probably the best part.  Even generally dislikeable figures are written in a way that makes them multi-dimentional, and you find yourself sympathizing even if they make bad choices along the way.

Wonderful characters

Reviewed at: Epilogue
by terrapin Friday, July 8, 2022 2:17:21 AM

rkgoff strikes again with a great mystery novel. Set in a generic Western country a century or so ago, the tone reminds me of the (admittedly few) classic novels I've read: very heavy on the showing rather than telling, and lots of focus on relationships and propriety. The synopsis sums it up very well, although it does sort of make it sound like there will be a massive conspiracy when it says important things are at stake, which isn't really true. In fact, I feel the mystery is secondary to the characters---like many good mystery novels, this one is as much about diving into the backstories of the involved parties and figuring out what set the stage for the crime as it is about actually solving the crime. But still, there's some good action and sleuthing scenes to lighten all the serious discussions and family secrets.

Style: 5/5

I think rkgoff did a good job writing as if they were from the same country and time as the book was set in. This is all the more impressive because the style differs a fair bit from their last story, but it is nonetheless consistent and smooth.

 

Story: 4/5

I think the actual "mystery" part could be fleshed out a bit more. Some of the conclusions seemed a bit abrupt and out of nowhere. But, this is something I dislike about a lot of mystery novels, where surprise endings are almost required. If you like mysteries, you'll probably like this. If you don't like mysteries, you'll probably like this for the family drama or the romance or the action. There's something for everyone.

 

Grammar: 5/5

It's great.

 

Character 5/5

My favorite part of the story. The show-don't-tell style does a great job fleshing out the ensemble cast, and I appreciate that the author stayed true to the characters' motivations, using them to drive the story instead of vice versa.

In particular I'm glad Penn didn't end up renouncing crime and living happily ever after with Eleanor. That sort of thing is all too common in similar plots, but a bit too incredible for my taste.
